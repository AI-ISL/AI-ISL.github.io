Abstract

Diffusion large language models (dLLMs) enable any-order generation, but this flexibility enlarges the attack surface: harmful spans may appear at arbitrary positions, and template-based prefilling attacks such as DIJA bypass responselevel refusals. We introduce A2D (Any-Order, Any-Step Defense), a token-level alignment method that aligns dLLMs to emit an [EOS] refusal signal whenever harmful content arises. By aligning safety directly at the token-level under randomized masking, A2D achieves robustness to both any-decoding-order and any-step prefilling attacks under various conditions. It also enables real-time monitoring: dLLMs may begin a response but automatically terminate if unsafe continuation emerges. On safety benchmarks, A2D consistently prevents the generation of harmful outputs, slashing DIJA success rates from over 80% to near-zero (1.3% on LLaDA-8B-Instruct, 0.0% on Dream-v0-Instruct-7B), and thresholded [EOS] probabilities allow early rejection, yielding up to 19.3× faster safe termination

Why A2D?

Traditional safety alignment methods were mostly developed for autoregressive (left-to-right) decoding. For diffusion decoding, safety needs to be robust under both any-order token generation and any-step vulnerability (unsafe spans can appear late, after partial generation).

  • Any-order: the next token can be generated at arbitrary positions (not only the leftmost).
  • Any-step: attacks can target later decoding steps after early refusals have “faded”.
  • Prefilling attacks: adversarial templates can interleave text and [MASK] tokens to bypass response-level refusal.

Method: Token-Level Alignment with [EOS]

A2D aligns safety at the token level. Intuitively, whenever masked tokens correspond to harmful spans, the model is trained to output [EOS] at those positions, producing an automatic refusal/termination signal that works under flexible decoding trajectories.

  • Step 1: Build two datasets: (a) harmful prompts with unsafe responses, (b) retain prompts/responses that should remain helpful.
  • Step 2: Sample a random mask pattern (and timestep / mask ratio) to create a partially-masked input.
  • Step 3: For masked tokens inside harmful spans, set targets to [EOS]; otherwise reconstruct original tokens.
  • Step 4: Train with the standard masked diffusion objective (same training “shape”, different targets).
  • Step 5: At inference, use P([EOS]) as an internal safety signal (optional early rejection / early termination).
